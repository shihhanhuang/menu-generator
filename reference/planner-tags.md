# Planner Tags and Attributes

These notes explain how to mark recipes so the planner behaves the way you expect.

## Weekly mood

Asian: Main ideas are biased toward recipes tagged Asian. Build-out suggestions then prefer Asian or Flexible add-ons once you choose an Asian main.

Western: Main ideas are biased toward recipes tagged Western. Build-out suggestions then prefer Western or Flexible add-ons once you choose a Western main.

## Repeat

Normal: Default behavior.

Often: Boosts the recipe. Use for household staples, reliable favorites, and meals you are happy to see frequently.

Rarely: Keeps the recipe available but makes it less likely. Use for recipes you like but do not want often.

Avoid: Strongly suppresses the recipe from planning. Use when you do not want it suggested, but do not necessarily want to hide it from your recipe list.

## Planner visibility

Hidden from planner: Removes the recipe from automatic suggestions and manual planner pickers. Use for recipes you do not currently want in the planning pool.

Hidden recipes can be found again with the Hidden filter in the Recipes tab.

## Meal role

Main: Can be used as a main lunch or dinner idea.

Add-on: Can be used to build out a meal, such as a salad, vegetable, carb, protein, appetizer, or flexible side.

Snack/dessert: Can be used when you mark snacks or dessert as needed.

A recipe can have more than one role. For example, a noodle dish could be Main and Add-on if you sometimes use it as one of several dishes.

## Add-on type

Flexible side: A general side that can work in several places. Use this when the recipe is not specifically a salad, vegetable, carb, protein, or appetizer, but you might want it to help build out a meal.

Appetizer: A starter, dip, small plate, or pre-dinner dish.

Salad: Any salad-like dish, including fruit salads, grain salads, slaws, and vegetable salads.

Vegetable: A vegetable side or vegetable-forward dish.

Carb: Rice, noodles, potatoes, bread, pasta, grains, or other starches used to round out a meal.

Protein: Meat, fish, tofu, eggs, beans, or another protein dish that could accompany another main.

A recipe can have multiple add-on types. For example, balsamic strawberry asparagus can be both Salad and Vegetable.

## Planning tags

Asian: Asian-style recipe or something that pairs naturally with Asian meals. Boosted when Weekly mood is Asian. Build-out suggestions prefer Asian or Flexible add-ons when the chosen main is Asian.

Western: Western-style recipe or something that pairs naturally with Western meals. Boosted when Weekly mood is Western. Build-out suggestions prefer Western or Flexible add-ons when the chosen main is Western.

Flexible: Cuisine-neutral or works easily with either Asian or Western meals. Useful for simple salads, rice, plain vegetables, fruit, or adaptable sides.

Easy: Low effort, low friction, or reliable on a tired day. Boosted for Easy mode and busy days.

Creative: Good when you want something interesting, new, playful, or a little more involved. Boosted in Creative mood.

Healthy: Good fit for a healthier-feeling week. Boosted in Healthy mood.

Busy day: Suitable for a busy dinner day. Boosted when that day is marked busy.

Weekend: Better when there is more time or attention. Boosted on Saturday and Sunday.

Hot day: Good for hot weather, warm-season eating, or meals that feel refreshing. Boosted when weather-aware inspiration is active and the seasonal weather is hot.

Light: Fresh, not too heavy, good for lighter weeks. Boosted in Light & fresh mood and also helps Healthy mood.

Cozy: Warming, comforting, rainy-day or winter-feeling food. Boosted in Cozy mood.

Spring: Good spring food or uses spring produce such as asparagus, rhubarb, peas, radishes, or mint. Boosted when seasonal inspiration is active in spring.

Summer: Good summer food or uses summer produce such as tomatoes, corn, zucchini, eggplant, cucumbers, berries, or peaches. Boosted when seasonal inspiration is active in summer.

Autumn: Good autumn food or uses autumn produce such as mushrooms, squash, pumpkin, apples, pears, or chestnuts. Boosted when seasonal inspiration is active in autumn.

Winter: Good winter food or uses winter cues such as stew, soup, braises, roasts, cabbage, leeks, root vegetables, or orange. Boosted when seasonal inspiration is active in winter.

Keeps: Keeps well, reheats well, works for leftovers, freezes, or can be made ahead. Helps especially in Use favorites and Minimal cooking moods.

Start early: Needs advance prep, marinating, chilling, soaking, proofing, long resting, or another early start. Penalized on busy days and in Minimal cooking/Easy mode.

Hands-on: Needs meaningful active cooking attention, lots of chopping, shaping, rolling, assembling, stirring, or close supervision. Penalized on busy days and in Minimal cooking/Easy mode.

Special occasion: Better for entertaining, family visits, celebrations, or times you want something more special. Slightly boosted on weekends.

## Tags that should usually be left blank

If a recipe is weather-agnostic or season-agnostic, do not select all seasons. Leave the season tags blank. Blank means flexible.

Use season tags only when season is a meaningful reason you would want the recipe.

## Practical examples

Fast pasta your family likes: Main, Often, Easy, Busy day.

A big stew: Main, Cozy, Winter, Keeps, maybe Start early.

Rice: Add-on, Carb, Easy, Busy day.

Asparagus salad: Add-on, Salad, Vegetable, Spring, Light, Hot day.

Fancy dessert: Snack/dessert, Special occasion, maybe Weekend.

Complicated dumplings: Main or Add-on depending how you use them, Creative, Weekend, Hands-on, maybe Start early.
